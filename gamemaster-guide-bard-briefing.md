## 1. The Copyright Problem (Why We Can't Just Use Publisher Content)

We identified a significant IP risk that changes how the demo is built and what we show publicly.

**The core issue:** Board game publishers own copyright on:
- Rulebook text — not the mechanics themselves (mechanics can't be copyrighted), but the specific written expression of those rules, examples, diagrams, and formatting
- Artwork — box art, card illustrations, board designs, component visuals
- Trademarks — game names and logos, which can't be used commercially without permission

Using any of this in a commercial product (which GameMaster Guide is — it's a paid subscription service) without a license is infringement. This applies even if we're "just teaching" the rules.

**The specific risk at Dice Tower West:** Publishers attend this convention. Demoing an app that reproduces their content without permission — in a room where they could walk up and see it — is both a legal and relationship risk we can't afford at this stage.

**What's safe without a license:**
- The AI explaining rules *in its own words* (synthesized from knowledge, not reproduced text)
- Game names and titles (lower risk, but still worth being careful with commercially)
- Publicly licensed or public domain content

**The path forward on licensing:** We've drafted outreach emails to Stonemaier Games (contact@stonemaier.com) and Asmodee North America (inquiries@asmodeena.com) to begin formal partnership conversations. Stonemaier is the highest-priority target — Jamey Stegmaier is known for being accessible and community-oriented, and a quick yes from him would be a meaningful credibility signal before the convention.

## 2. The Demo Pivot: Public Domain Games Only at Dice Tower West

For the convention demo, we are pivoting the front-facing product to use **fully public domain games only**. No licensed publisher content will appear in any screenshot, demo screen, or marketing material shown at the event.

**Why this is actually a smart move, not just a safe one:**
- Zero IP ambiguity — we can show anything freely, print it, put it on a tablet, hand it to a publisher
- Public domain games are universally understood — Chess, Checkers, Go — no explanation needed
- It lets the *product* speak, not the content: publishers can immediately see how it would work with their games
- Clean separation between "what we're showing" and "what we're asking permission for"

**The approved public domain game list for the demo:**

| Game | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Chess | Best anchor — universally recognized, complex enough to show teaching depth |
| Checkers | Simple, great for showing onboarding flow |
| Go | Strategic, appeals to the hobby gamer crowd at the con |
| Backgammon | Classic, broad appeal |
| Mancala | Accessible, good casual player demo |
| Mahjong | Traditional tile game (not any branded edition) |
| Reversi | Note: call it Reversi, NOT Othello — Othello is a Mattel trademark |
| Dominoes | Simple rules, shows the teaching format clearly |
| Pachisi | The original game Ludo/Parcheesi derives from |

**The pitch framing when talking to publishers:**  
*"Here's how it works on Chess — now imagine this with [their game]."*  
This is actually more compelling than showing their game without permission, because it puts them in the driver's seat of imagining the partnership.

## 3. The Publisher Outreach Strategy at Dice Tower West

**The approach: Be a regular attendee, not a vendor.**

We are not paying for a booth. We are not approaching publishers as a startup pitching a product. We are going as engaged community members who happen to be building something relevant — and using casual, organic conversations to plant seeds.

**Why this framing works:**
- Publishers at conventions are constantly pitched by vendors and developers — they have their guard up at booths
- A genuine conversation on the floor, at a panel, or in the game library is disarming and human
- It positions us as part of the community, not extracting from it
- It gives them an easy out ("send me a follow-up email") which is actually what we want — a warm email is more actionable than a business card

**The conversation flow:**

1. **Start as a fan/player.** Engage with their games genuinely. Ask about upcoming releases, what they're demoing.
2. **Mention the problem naturally.** *"Do you find that cafes struggle to teach your games to new customers? That's actually something I've been working on..."*
3. **Show, don't pitch.** Pull out a phone or tablet with the Chess/public domain demo running. Let them interact with it.
4. **Plant the idea, not the ask.** *"I'd love to get [Game Name] in here eventually — that would require working with you on content rights. Is there someone at [Publisher] I should talk to about that?"*
5. **Get a name and follow up by email within 24 hours.**

**Priority targets at the convention:**

| Publisher | Games | Why They Matter |
|-----------|-------|-----------------|
| Stonemaier Games | Wingspan, Scythe, Viticulture | Community-first culture, Jamey is accessible, high café popularity |
| Asmodee (any imprint) | Ticket to Ride, Catan, Pandemic, Carcassonne | Largest publisher, most recognizable titles for casual players — exactly our audience |
| CMON | Zombicide, Blood Rage | Popular at cafés, visual-heavy games that benefit from guided teaching |
| Indie publishers (any) | Varies | Smaller publishers are more likely to say yes quickly with minimal legal overhead |

**What we're NOT doing:**
- Claiming we already have licensing
- Showing any of their game content in the demo without permission
- Pitching subscription prices or business terms on the floor
- Handing out sales materials that imply publisher endorsement

**What we ARE doing:**
- Building relationships that turn into warm email conversations after the convention
- Collecting names of the right contacts at each publisher
- Validating that publishers see the problem we're solving
- Establishing GameMaster Guide as a credible, community-oriented product before we ask for anything
