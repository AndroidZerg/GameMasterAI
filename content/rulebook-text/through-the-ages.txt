# Game: Through the Ages: A New Story of Civilization
# Publisher: Czech Games Edition
# Players: 2-4 | Time: 120-240 min | Weight: 4.4/5

## OVERVIEW
Through the Ages is a civilization-building card game where players guide a civilization from antiquity through the modern era. Players develop technologies, construct wonders, elect leaders, and build armies — all without a map. The game emphasizes resource management, long-term planning, and balancing military strength against cultural output. Victory goes to the civilization with the most culture points at game's end.

## COMPONENTS
- Civil and Military card decks (Age A, Age I, Age II, Age III)
- Player boards (production/government tracking)
- Yellow and blue tokens (workers and unused workers)
- White and red tokens (food, resources, science, culture markers)
- Military cards (tactics, aggressions, wars, pacts, events)
- Culture and Science tracks
- Card row (13-card display)
- Event deck

## SETUP
1. Give each player a player board, starting government (Despotism), starting leader slot, and initial tokens.
2. Each player begins with 1 Bronze Mine (producing 2 resources), 2 Farms (producing 2 food each = 4 food), 1 Warrior, 1 Lab (producing 1 science), 1 Philosophy (producing 1 culture).
3. Players start with 18 unused worker tokens (yellow) in the yellow bank; blue tokens represent workers on cards.
4. Populate the card row with 13 cards from the Age A deck.
5. Place Age A military cards into each player's hand (varies by player count).
6. Set starting culture to 0 and starting science to 0.
7. First player gets fewer civil actions (1) in the opening round.

## GAMEPLAY / TURN STRUCTURE

The game is played over four ages (A, I, II, III). Each player's turn consists of these phases:

### 1. Political Phase (skipped in Age A)
- **Events**: Resolve the current event (drawn previously). Then play a new event card face-down from your military hand onto the future events deck.
- **Aggressions & Wars**: You may play aggression cards or declare war against other players. The defender can play defense cards. Compare military strength; the loser suffers the aggression's penalty.
- **Pacts**: You may propose pacts to other players (mutual benefits).

### 2. Action Phase
You have a limited number of **Civil Actions** (white dots) and **Military Actions** (red dots), determined by your government type.

**Civil Actions** (each costs 1 civil action unless noted):
- **Take a card from the card row**: Cards in positions 1-5 cost 1 civil action; positions 6-9 cost 2; positions 10-13 cost 3. Cards include technologies, wonders, leaders, and action cards.
- **Increase population**: Pay food equal to the food cost shown on your player board (increases as you get more workers). Move a yellow token from the yellow bank to the pool of available workers.
- **Build/Upgrade a building**: Pay the resource cost to place a worker on a technology card (farm, mine, lab, temple, arena, theater, library). Upgrading replaces a worker on an older version with the newer version and costs the difference.
- **Build a Wonder stage**: Pay the required resources to advance one stage on a wonder under construction. Only one wonder may be under construction at a time.
- **Play an Action Card**: Some civil cards are one-time actions.
- **Change Government**: Revolution (costs science, no action cost but replaces your actions for the turn) or Peaceful Transition (costs more science but uses a civil action and lets you keep acting).
- **Elect a Leader**: Play a leader card (only one leader at a time; old leader is discarded).

**Military Actions** (each costs 1 military action):
- **Build military units** (pay resources, place workers).
- **Upgrade military units**.
- **Play Tactics cards** (change army formation for bonuses).
- **Draw Military cards** (draw cards from the military deck).

### 3. Production Phase (automatic)
- **Corruption check**: If your resources/food exceed your storage capacity, you lose the excess.
- **Produce food**: Each farm produces food.
- **Consume food**: Pay food equal to consumption (based on population). If you can't pay, there's an uprising — you lose 4 culture per missing food.
- **Produce resources**: Each mine produces resources.
- **Produce science**: Each lab produces science.
- **Produce culture**: Each temple, theater, and other culture buildings produce culture. Culture is added to your culture track.

### 4. Maintenance Phase
- Discard military cards down to your hand limit (based on military technology).
- Refill the card row: discard the cheapest cards and slide remaining cards left, then draw new cards from the current age deck.

### Age Transitions
When the Age I, II, or III deck is introduced, the corresponding older age cards are gradually removed from the card row. The game ends at the end of the round when the Age III deck runs out.

## SCORING / WINNING
- **Culture points** are the sole victory condition. The player with the most culture at game end wins.
- Culture is earned throughout the game via: culture-producing buildings (temples, theaters), wonders, leaders, events, territory bonuses, and end-game scoring events.
- Age III events often award large culture bonuses based on specific criteria (most military, most technology, etc.).
- Military is not scored directly but protects you from aggressions/wars (which can steal culture or destroy buildings).

## KEY RULES & TIPS
- **Corruption matters**: If you produce more resources or food than you can store, you lose the excess. Build granaries and warehouses to increase capacity.
- **Military neglect is punished**: Even if you focus on economy, having the weakest military invites devastating aggressions. Maintain at least a competitive military.
- **Food consumption scales**: The cost to feed your population increases with each new worker (2, 3, 4, 5, 7...). Don't over-expand population without upgrading farms.
- **Leaders are temporary**: Leaders are replaced when you elect a new one or when the age changes. Plan around their limited tenure.
- **Revolution vs. Peaceful Transition**: Revolution is cheaper in science but ends your civil actions for the turn. Peaceful transition costs more science but lets you keep taking actions.
- **Card row timing**: Valuable cards in expensive positions (right side) may be worth the extra civil action cost if they're critical to your strategy.
- **Wonders must be completed before the next age's wonders become available**, or they are discarded incomplete.
- **Events are seeded by players**: The event deck contains cards players chose to put there. Pay attention to what opponents might have seeded.
- **Common mistake**: Forgetting that upgrading a building costs the difference in resources between the old and new version, not the full cost.
- **Common mistake**: Forgetting corruption — always check storage limits before producing.
