# Players: 2-6 | Time: 120-240 min | Weight: 3.7/5

## OVERVIEW
Eclipse: Second Dawn for the Galaxy is a 4X (explore, expand, exploit, exterminate) space civilization game. Players control interstellar civilizations exploring new star systems, researching technologies, building fleets, and engaging in epic space battles. The game elegantly integrates an economic system where every action you take increases your civilization's upkeep cost, creating tense decisions about expansion versus sustainability.

## COMPONENTS
- Hex tiles (star systems with planets of various types)
- Player boards with action tracks, population cubes, influence discs, and storage tracks
- Ship blueprints (Interceptor, Cruiser, Dreadnought, Starbase) with customizable component slots
- Ship miniatures (in player colors)
- Technology tiles (Military, Grid, Nano categories)
- Discovery tiles
- Reputation tiles (for combat victories)
- Dice (custom combat dice in orange, yellow, red, blue)
- Ambassador tiles
- Ancient/GCDS/Guardian ship tiles
- Orbital and Monolith pieces
- Colony ships, resource cubes (Money/orange, Science/pink, Materials/brown)
- Traitor card

## SETUP
1. Place the Galactic Center hex (with the GCDS — Galactic Center Defense System) in the middle of the table.
2. Each player takes a player board, starting hex tile (home system), 3 starting ships (1 Interceptor), and their influence discs, population cubes, and ship blueprints.
3. Players place population cubes on their home system planets (covering resource tracks on their player board — removing cubes reveals higher income).
4. Set out the technology supply, sorted into 3 categories (Military, Grid, Nano) and 3 tiers (cost 2, 4, 6+).
5. Create hex tile stacks for each ring (inner, middle, outer sectors).
6. Each player starts with specific resources depending on their faction (humans start with 2 Money, 3 Science, 3 Materials).
7. Place reputation tiles face-down in draw piles by value.

## GAMEPLAY / TURN STRUCTURE

The game is played over 9 rounds. Each round has an Action Phase and a subsequent set of end-round phases.

### Action Phase
Players take turns in order, performing one action per turn, cycling around the table. Each action requires placing an influence disc from your supply onto that action's track on your player board. As you take more actions, your upkeep cost (shown on the influence track) increases. You can pass to stop taking actions for the round; you cannot take more actions after passing.

**Available Actions:**

1. **Explore**: Draw hex tiles from the appropriate ring stack, choose one to place adjacent to your controlled hexes. You may then place an influence disc on the new hex to claim it. Hexes contain planets (for colonization), discovery tokens, wormhole connections, and possibly ancient ships to fight.

2. **Influence**: Move up to 2 influence discs — place them on unoccupied hexes to claim them, or remove them from hexes to abandon systems (reducing upkeep). You may also flip colony ships to colonize planet slots.

3. **Research**: Take a technology tile from the supply by paying its science cost (reduced by your science income). Place it on your player board. Technologies include weapons (Ion Cannon, Plasma Missile), drives (Fusion, Tachyon), computers, shields, hulls, and special techs (Neutron Bombs, Wormhole Generator, Cloaking Device, etc.).

4. **Upgrade**: Place up to 2 component tiles onto your ship blueprints. Each ship type (Interceptor, Cruiser, Dreadnought, Starbase) has a blueprint with component slots. You can customize ships with weapons, shields, drives, computers, hull, and other tech you've researched. All existing ships of that type immediately gain the upgraded blueprint.

5. **Build**: Build up to 2 ships and/or structures (Orbitals, Monoliths) in systems you control, paying the material cost. Ship costs: Interceptor = 3, Cruiser = 5, Dreadnought = 8, Starbase = 3. Orbitals cost 4 and give +1 money. Monoliths cost 10 and give 3 VP.

6. **Move**: Move up to 3 ships, each up to their blueprint's move value, through connected wormholes. Ships entering a hex with enemy ships trigger combat (resolved later). Pinning rule: if opponent ships are in your hex, your ships must stay.

### Combat Phase (end of round)
All battles are resolved simultaneously across the galaxy. Combat occurs in the system where opposing ships coexist.

**Combat Resolution:**
1. Ships fire in initiative order (highest initiative value first).
2. Roll dice based on weapons: Ion Cannons = orange dice, Plasma Missiles = red dice, Soliton Cannons = yellow dice, Antimatter Cannons = blue dice.
3. Add your computer value to each die roll. If the total meets or exceeds 6, it's a hit.
4. Defender subtracts shield value from hits.
5. Hits remove hull points. Ships with 0 hull remaining are destroyed.
6. Missiles fire only in the first round of combat.
7. Combat continues in rounds until one side is eliminated or retreats.

The winner draws reputation tiles (VP) based on the most expensive enemy ship destroyed.

### Upkeep Phase
- Pay money equal to your upkeep cost (determined by how many influence discs you've used). If you can't pay, you must remove discs (abandon systems) until you can.
- Produce resources: Reveal resource values by having colonized planets (cubes removed from tracks show income levels).

### Cleanup Phase
- Move colony ships to hexes where needed.
- Return action discs.
- Pass the start player marker.

## SCORING / WINNING
After 9 rounds, score victory points:
- **Reputation tiles** earned from combat victories (2, 3, or 4 VP each)
- **Ambassador tiles** (1 VP each for diplomatic relations)
- **Controlled hexes** (1-4 VP each, depending on hex value)
- **Discovery tiles** kept as VP (worth 2 VP each if not used for their special ability)
- **Monoliths** (3 VP each)
- **Technology track bonuses** (having many techs in one category scores VP)
- **Traitor card** (-2 VP if you broke a diplomatic relation by attacking an ally)
- Highest total VP wins. Tiebreaker: most resources remaining.

## KEY RULES & TIPS
- **Economy is everything**: Every influence disc you place increases your upkeep. Expanding too fast without sufficient money income will bankrupt you. Always watch your income vs. upkeep balance.
- **Ship customization is key**: Don't neglect the Upgrade action. A well-designed Interceptor with computers and good weapons can be more cost-effective than a default Dreadnought.
- **Computers vs. Shields**: Computers add to hit rolls (making it easier to hit 6+), while shields subtract from incoming hits. In an arms race, computers tend to beat shields.
- **Missiles fire only in Round 1**: Plasma Missiles are powerful but only fire in the first combat round. If the battle drags on, missile-heavy ships become much weaker.
- **Ancient ships guard discoveries**: Inner-ring hexes often contain Ancients — defeating them gives valuable discovery tiles and reputation.
- **Common mistake**: Forgetting that ALL ships of a type change when you upgrade a blueprint. You can't have two different Interceptor designs.
- **Common mistake**: Moving ships through hexes without wormhole connections. Ships can only move through connected wormholes.
- **Diplomacy**: Exchanging ambassador tiles grants VP but also means attacking that player costs you the Traitor card (-2 VP). Weigh carefully.
- **The GCDS (Galactic Center)**: The center hex is guarded by a powerful ancient ship. Defeating it grants significant VP but requires a strong fleet.
