## OVERVIEW
Boonlake is a heavy euro-style strategy game designed by Alexander Pfister where players settle and develop the area around a newly discovered lake. Players use a unique action-selection rondel combined with multi-use cards to build settlements, explore the landscape, manage resources, and develop their personal boards. The game rewards careful planning across multiple interconnected systems including ship movement, landscape development, and community building.

## COMPONENTS
- 1 main game board (featuring the rondel track, lake, landscape area, and community tracks)
- 4 player boards (double-sided for different difficulty levels)
- ~120 multi-use project cards
- Resource tokens: wood, clay, stone, food, coins
- Settlement pieces (houses) in 4 player colors
- Ship tokens and ship track
- Landscape tiles
- Community board with advancement tracks
- Exploration tokens
- Person tiles (workers for your player board)
- Solo mode components (Automa deck)
- Score track

## SETUP
1. Place the main board in the center. Place resource supplies nearby.
2. Populate the landscape area with landscape tiles according to the setup diagram.
3. Place exploration tokens on designated spaces on the board.
4. Set up the community board with starting positions for each track.
5. Shuffle the project card deck; deal a market row of face-up cards.
6. Each player takes a player board, starting resources (typically: some combination of wood, clay, stone, food, and coins — varies by player order), starting settlements, and a ship marker.
7. Place each player's ship on the starting position of the ship track.
8. Place each player's scoring marker at 0 on the score track.
9. Deal each player a starting hand of project cards.
10. Determine starting player and give them the first-player marker.

## GAMEPLAY / TURN STRUCTURE
The game is played over a series of rounds until a game-end condition is triggered (certain number of project cards drawn, or specific board conditions met).

### On Your Turn
You must perform ONE main action by selecting an action space on the rondel:

#### The Rondel
- The main board features a rondel (circular action track) with multiple action spaces.
- You move your marker FORWARD (clockwise) on the rondel to the action you want to take.
- Moving further ahead costs more (you may need to pay coins or skip actions).
- You CANNOT move backward. You cannot stay on the same space.
- When your marker completes a full circuit, a new round phase triggers for you.

#### Action Spaces on the Rondel

**1. Settle**
- Place settlements (houses) from your player board onto the landscape on the main board.
- You pay the resource cost shown (varies by terrain: forest costs wood, hills cost clay, mountains cost stone, etc.).
- Removing houses from your player board may reveal bonuses underneath.
- Settlements must typically be placed adjacent to the lake or connected to existing settlements via roads/paths.

**2. Explore**
- Move your ship along the lake.
- Collect exploration tokens from spaces your ship passes through or lands on.
- Exploration tokens provide immediate resources, victory points, or special abilities.
- Ship movement may cost food.

**3. Build/Develop**
- Develop landscape tiles on the main board — flip or upgrade tiles.
- Developed tiles provide ongoing benefits or end-game scoring.
- Costs resources depending on the development type.

**4. Take a Project Card**
- Draft a project card from the market row into your hand.
- Project cards are multi-use: they can be played for their top action, used for their resource/cost contribution, or saved for end-game scoring.

**5. Play a Project Card**
- Play a project card from your hand for its main effect.
- Effects vary widely: gain resources, place settlements, advance on community tracks, score points, gain special abilities.

**6. Community**
- Advance on one of the community tracks on the community board.
- Community tracks represent shared development of the area: infrastructure, culture, prosperity, etc.
- Advancing requires specific resources or conditions.
- Reaching milestones on these tracks provides bonuses and end-game scoring opportunities.
- Your position relative to other players on these tracks matters for scoring.

### Multi-Use Cards (Key Mechanism)
- Every project card can be used in multiple ways:
  - **Primary action**: Play it for its main printed effect.
  - **Support**: Discard it to boost another action (pay part of a cost, gain an extra benefit).
  - **End-game scoring**: Some cards score bonus points if conditions are met at game end.
- Managing your hand of cards — deciding when to play, discard, or save — is central to the strategy.

### Person Tiles
- Person tiles are acquired through gameplay and placed on your player board.
- They provide ongoing abilities, resource generation, or end-game scoring.
- Your player board has limited slots for persons — choose wisely.

## SCORING / WINNING
The game ends after a set number of rounds or when the project card deck runs out (exact trigger depends on player count).

**Final Scoring includes:**
- Points accumulated during the game (from actions, exploration, settlements).
- Community track positions: scored based on rank relative to other players.
- Developed landscape tiles: bonus points for specific configurations.
- Person tiles: end-game scoring from workers on your player board.
- Project cards: any held cards with end-game scoring conditions.
- Settlements on the board: points based on location and connections.
- Remaining resources may convert to points at a low rate.

The player with the most victory points wins. Tiebreaker: most remaining resources.

## KEY RULES & TIPS
- The rondel is the heart of the game — moving too far ahead costs you tempo, but the right action at the right time is worth the cost. Balance efficiency with timing.
- Multi-use cards create agonizing decisions: a card perfect for end-game scoring might be exactly what you need to discard for resources now.
- Settling early unlocks player board bonuses (revealed by removing houses), which compound over the game.
- Exploration (ship movement) provides valuable one-time bonuses — don't neglect it, especially early.
- Community tracks are a major source of end-game points. Falling too far behind other players on key tracks is costly.
- Resource management is tight. Plan 2-3 turns ahead to ensure you can afford your desired actions.
- Person tiles provide powerful engine-building capabilities — acquire them early to benefit across more turns.
- In multiplayer, pay attention to which actions opponents are taking on the rondel; if they skip ahead, you may get cheap access to actions they passed.
- The solo mode uses an Automa opponent that competes on community tracks and blocks board spaces.
- First play will likely run closer to 150 minutes; experienced players can finish in 90-100.
