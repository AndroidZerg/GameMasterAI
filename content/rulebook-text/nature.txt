# Game: Nature
# Publisher: North Star Games
# Players: 2-5 | Time: 45-60 min | Weight: 1.8/5

## OVERVIEW
Nature is an engine-building card game where players create thriving ecosystems by playing animal, plant, and terrain cards into a personal tableau. Often compared to Wingspan for its accessible nature theme and card-driven engine building, Nature focuses on building food chains and habitats. Players draft cards and manage resources to attract wildlife and score points through ecological synergies.

## COMPONENTS
- Large deck of Nature cards (animals, plants, terrain/habitat cards)
- Player boards (personal ecosystem tableaus with habitat rows)
- Resource tokens (food tokens of various types — seeds, insects, fish, meat, nectar)
- Dice (for food production or action selection)
- Score pad
- First player token
- Reference cards

## SETUP
1. Give each player a personal player board (ecosystem mat).
2. Shuffle the Nature card deck and deal each player a starting hand of cards (typically 5-8 cards; players may keep some and discard others).
3. Set up the central supply of food tokens.
4. Place the shared card display (a row of face-up cards available for drafting).
5. Each player receives starting food resources as indicated.
6. Determine the first player randomly.

## GAMEPLAY / TURN STRUCTURE
The game is played over a series of rounds. Each round, players take turns performing actions:

### On Your Turn
Choose one main action:
1. **Play a Card**: Pay the food cost shown on a Nature card from your hand and place it into the matching habitat row on your player board. Cards have:
   - **Habitat requirements**: Each card belongs to a specific ecosystem type (forest, wetland, grassland, etc.) and must be placed in the correct row.
   - **Food cost**: Pay the required food tokens to play the card.
   - **Abilities**: Many cards have "When Played," "Once Per Round," or "End of Game" abilities that generate food, draw cards, or score points.
   - **Food chain synergies**: Predators score more when prey animals are in your ecosystem; plants support herbivores, etc.

2. **Gain Food**: Take food tokens from the supply. The amount and type depends on your board state (terrain cards and certain animals produce food).

3. **Draw Cards**: Draw cards from the face-up display or the top of the deck to refill your hand.

### Round Structure
- After all players have taken a set number of turns per round (varies by round — typically decreasing as the game progresses, similar to Wingspan's structure), the round ends.
- Score any end-of-round bonuses.
- Refresh the card display.
- Pass the first player token.

### Card Types
- **Terrain/Habitat cards**: Provide ongoing food production and expand your ecosystem capacity.
- **Plant cards**: Low cost, provide food for herbivores, often have passive abilities.
- **Herbivore cards**: Mid-cost animals that benefit from plants in your tableau.
- **Predator cards**: Higher cost, score big points but need prey animals present.
- **Special/Bonus cards**: Provide unique scoring conditions or powerful one-time effects.

## SCORING / WINNING
At the end of the final round, players total their victory points from:
- **Card point values**: Each card in your ecosystem has a base VP value.
- **Food chain bonuses**: Predators score additional points for having appropriate prey; herbivores score for having plants, etc.
- **End-of-game card abilities**: Cards with end-game scoring conditions.
- **Round bonus objectives**: Points earned from meeting round-specific goals.
- **Leftover resources**: Some resources may convert to points.

The player with the most victory points wins. Ties broken by most cards in ecosystem, then most remaining food.

## KEY RULES & TIPS
- Cards must be placed in the correct habitat row — you cannot place a wetland bird in a forest row.
- Food chain connections are the primary scoring engine. Build coherent food webs: plants feed herbivores, herbivores feed predators.
- The number of actions per round decreases as the game progresses (similar to Wingspan), so early rounds offer more flexibility.
- Card drafting from the shared display is important — watch what opponents need and consider hate-drafting.
- Balance between playing cards (building your engine) and gaining food (fueling future plays).
- Terrain cards are valuable early because they provide ongoing food production throughout the game.
- Do not neglect card draw — running out of good options limits your scoring potential.
- Each game feels different due to the large card variety. Adapt your strategy to the cards you draw rather than forcing a specific plan.
- At higher player counts, the card display refreshes more frequently, creating more competition for key cards.
