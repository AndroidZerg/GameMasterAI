# Game: Concept
# Publisher: Repos Production
# Players: 4-12 | Time: 40 min | Weight: 1.4/5

## OVERVIEW
Concept is a party guessing game where players use a board full of universal icons to communicate words, phrases, or concepts without speaking. The active team places colored pawns and cubes on icons to build associations — combining ideas like "big," "yellow," "food" to get someone to guess "banana." It is a creative, language-independent deduction game that works well with large groups.

## COMPONENTS
- 1 large game board covered with universal icons (roughly 100+ icons representing concepts like: color, size, location, material, shape, function, era, fictional/real, animal, person, object, etc.)
- 110 Concept cards (double-sided), each with 9 concepts to guess (3 easy in blue, 3 medium in red, 3 hard in black)
- Pawns and cubes in multiple colors:
  - 1 green question mark pawn (main concept) + green cubes
  - 1 orange exclamation pawn (sub-concept 1) + orange cubes
  - 1 blue exclamation pawn (sub-concept 2) + blue cubes
  - 1 red exclamation pawn (sub-concept 3) + red cubes
  - Additional yellow cubes (sub-concept 4)
- Light bulb tokens (victory points)
- Storage bowls for pieces

## SETUP
1. Place the game board in the center of the table where everyone can see it.
2. Sort all pawns and cubes by color into the storage bowls.
3. Shuffle the Concept cards and place them in a face-down pile.
4. Place the light bulb tokens nearby.
5. Form teams of two (the "clue givers") who will sit next to each other. Other players are guessers.

## GAMEPLAY / TURN STRUCTURE

### Round Structure
1. **Two active players** form the clue-giving team for this round. They draw a Concept card and secretly agree on one of the 9 words/phrases to convey.
2. **The difficulty** is the team's choice (easy/medium/hard).

### Giving Clues
The clue-giving team places pieces on the board to describe their concept. No speaking, gestures, or sounds allowed from the clue givers — only placing and moving pieces on the board.

- **Green question mark pawn**: Place this on the icon that represents the MAIN concept (the primary category or defining feature). Place green cubes on additional icons that further describe the main concept.
- **Orange/Blue/Red pawns**: Use these to define SUB-CONCEPTS — related but distinct ideas that together help identify the answer. Each sub-concept gets its own pawn on its primary icon and cubes on supporting icons.

**Example**: To convey "Statue of Liberty":
- Green pawn on "building/monument" icon, green cube on "very large," green cube on "famous"
- Orange pawn on "location" icon, orange cube on "USA" or "island"
- Blue pawn on "person/woman" icon, blue cube on "holding something," blue cube on "green (color)"

### Guessing
- All other players shout out guesses at any time (no turn order for guessing).
- The clue givers nod "yes" or shake "no" to indicate if a guess is correct or on the right track.
- Clue givers can add, remove, or rearrange pieces on the board as guesses come in to redirect guessers.
- Once someone guesses correctly, the round ends.

### Scoring
- The player who guessed correctly receives 2 light bulb tokens.
- Each member of the clue-giving team receives 1 light bulb token.
- If no one guesses correctly after a reasonable time (group decides), no points are awarded and play passes to the next team.

### Next Round
- The clue-giving duty rotates clockwise. The next pair of adjacent players becomes the new clue-giving team.
- Clear all pieces from the board.

## SCORING / WINNING
- The game is intentionally casual about scoring. The rules state that the game can be played cooperatively without scoring at all.
- If playing competitively, the player with the most light bulb tokens after a set number of rounds (or when the group decides to stop) wins.
- Many groups simply play for fun without tracking score.

## KEY RULES & TIPS
- Clue givers CANNOT speak, point at objects in the room, make gestures, or make sounds. Only board piece placement is allowed.
- The distinction between the main concept (green) and sub-concepts (other colors) is key. Use sub-concepts to break complex ideas into components.
- Start with the most defining feature. For a person, start with gender/era/fame. For an object, start with category/size/material.
- The icons on the board are intentionally broad and open to interpretation. "Star" could mean celebrity, shape, space, rating, etc.
- Color icons are very useful — many concepts can be narrowed down quickly by color.
- You can remove and reposition pieces at any time. If guessers are going down the wrong path, reconfigure your clues.
- The game scales exceptionally well — it works from 4 to 12+ players. Larger groups create more chaotic and entertaining guessing.
- There is no time limit in the rules, but groups often impose one (3-5 minutes per concept) to keep the game moving.
- The hard (black) concepts often include abstract ideas, movie titles, or famous quotes that require creative use of the icons.
- The game is language-independent in theory (icons are universal), but cultural knowledge affects how easy concepts are to convey and guess.
- Concept works brilliantly as a team-building or ice-breaker game due to its accessibility and the "aha!" moments it creates.
