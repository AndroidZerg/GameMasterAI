## OVERVIEW
Chess is the world's most famous abstract strategy game, played for over 1,500 years. Two players control armies of 16 pieces each on an 8x8 board, maneuvering to checkmate the opponent's King. Checkers (also known as Draughts) is a simpler abstract game played on the same board where players capture opponent pieces by jumping over them. This Heirloom Edition provides premium components for both classic games.

# CHESS

## COMPONENTS
- 1 game board (8x8 alternating light/dark squares, columns labeled a-h, rows labeled 1-8)
- 16 White pieces: 1 King, 1 Queen, 2 Rooks, 2 Bishops, 2 Knights, 8 Pawns
- 16 Black pieces: 1 King, 1 Queen, 2 Rooks, 2 Bishops, 2 Knights, 8 Pawns

## SETUP
1. Orient the board so each player has a light-colored square in their right-hand corner.
2. Place pieces on the two rows closest to each player:
   - **Back row (row 1 for White, row 8 for Black)** from left to right: Rook, Knight, Bishop, Queen, King, Bishop, Knight, Rook.
   - **Memory aid**: The Queen goes on her own color (White Queen on light square, Black Queen on dark square).
   - **Front row (row 2 for White, row 7 for Black)**: All 8 Pawns.
3. White moves first.

## GAMEPLAY / TURN STRUCTURE
Players alternate turns. On your turn, you MUST move exactly one piece (with one exception: castling moves two pieces).

### Piece Movement

**King** (most important piece)
- Moves exactly 1 square in any direction (horizontal, vertical, diagonal).
- Cannot move into a square attacked by an opponent's piece ("into check").
- Special move: Castling (see below).

**Queen** (most powerful piece)
- Moves any number of squares in any single direction: horizontal, vertical, or diagonal.
- Cannot jump over other pieces.

**Rook**
- Moves any number of squares horizontally or vertically.
- Cannot jump over other pieces.
- Participates in Castling.

**Bishop**
- Moves any number of squares diagonally.
- Cannot jump over other pieces.
- Each Bishop is confined to one color of square for the entire game.

**Knight**
- Moves in an "L" shape: 2 squares in one direction + 1 square perpendicular (or vice versa).
- The ONLY piece that can jump over other pieces.
- Always lands on the opposite color square from where it started.

**Pawn**
- Moves forward 1 square (toward the opponent's side). Never moves backward.
- On its FIRST move, a Pawn may move forward 2 squares.
- Captures diagonally: 1 square forward-diagonally. Pawns do NOT capture straight ahead.
- Special move: En passant (see below).
- Special move: Promotion (see below).

### Capturing
- Move your piece to a square occupied by an opponent's piece. The opponent's piece is removed from the board.
- All pieces capture the same way they move, EXCEPT Pawns (which capture diagonally but move straight).
- Capturing is never mandatory in chess (unlike checkers).

### Special Moves

**Castling**
- A combined move of the King and one Rook. The only time two pieces move in one turn.
- The King moves 2 squares toward a Rook, and that Rook moves to the square the King crossed over.
- **Kingside castling**: King moves from e1 to g1, Rook moves from h1 to f1.
- **Queenside castling**: King moves from e1 to c1, Rook moves from a1 to d1.
- **Conditions** (ALL must be met): Neither the King nor that Rook has previously moved; no pieces between them; the King is not currently in check; the King does not pass through or land on an attacked square.

**En Passant**
- If an opponent's Pawn moves 2 squares forward from its starting position and lands beside your Pawn, you may capture it as if it had moved only 1 square.
- This capture must be made immediately on the very next turn or the right is lost.

**Pawn Promotion**
- When a Pawn reaches the opponent's back row (row 8 for White, row 1 for Black), it MUST be promoted.
- Replace it with a Queen, Rook, Bishop, or Knight of the same color (player's choice).
- Queen is almost always chosen. You may have multiple Queens.

### Check, Checkmate, and Draws

**Check**: Your King is under attack by an opponent's piece. You MUST resolve the check on your turn by: (a) moving the King, (b) capturing the attacking piece, or (c) blocking the attack with another piece.

**Checkmate**: Your King is in check and there is no legal move to escape. The game ends immediately — the player who delivered checkmate wins.

**Stalemate (Draw)**: The player whose turn it is has NO legal moves but is NOT in check. The game is a draw.

**Other Draw Conditions**:
- Agreement: Both players agree to draw.
- Threefold repetition: The same position occurs 3 times with the same player to move.
- 50-move rule: 50 consecutive moves by each side without a Pawn move or capture.
- Insufficient material: Neither player has enough pieces to force checkmate (e.g., King vs. King, King+Bishop vs. King).

## SCORING / WINNING
- **Win**: Checkmate your opponent's King, or opponent resigns.
- **Draw**: Stalemate, agreement, threefold repetition, 50-move rule, or insufficient material.
- **Piece values** (for strategic evaluation, not scoring): Pawn = 1, Knight = 3, Bishop = 3, Rook = 5, Queen = 9, King = invaluable.

## KEY RULES & TIPS
- Control the center (squares d4, d5, e4, e5) early — pieces are most powerful there.
- Develop pieces (Knights and Bishops) before launching attacks. Don't move the same piece repeatedly in the opening.
- Castle early to protect your King and connect your Rooks.
- Don't bring your Queen out too early — it can be chased by less valuable pieces, wasting your tempo.
- Rooks are strongest on open files (columns with no Pawns) and on the 7th rank.
- Knights are better in closed positions (lots of Pawns); Bishops are better in open positions.
- In endgames, the King becomes an active piece — bring it to the center.
- Always check for your opponent's threats before making your move.

# CHECKERS (DRAUGHTS)

## OVERVIEW
Checkers is played on the dark squares of an 8x8 board. Each player starts with 12 pieces. The goal is to capture all opponent pieces or block them from moving.

## SETUP
1. Use only the dark squares.
2. Each player places 12 pieces on the dark squares of their 3 closest rows (rows 1-3 and 6-8).
3. The middle two rows (4-5) start empty.
4. Black (or darker color) traditionally moves first.

## GAMEPLAY
- Pieces move diagonally forward 1 square to an adjacent dark square.
- **Capturing**: If an opponent's piece is diagonally adjacent and the square beyond it is empty, you MUST jump over it (capture it). Captured pieces are removed.
- **Multiple jumps**: If after a capture another jump is available, you MUST continue jumping in the same turn.
- **Mandatory captures**: If a capture is available, you MUST take it. If multiple captures are available, you may choose which one (but must complete all jumps in a chain).
- **Kinging**: When a piece reaches the opponent's back row, it becomes a King (stack a second piece on top). Kings can move AND capture diagonally in both directions (forward and backward).

## WINNING
- Capture all of your opponent's pieces, OR
- Block all opponent pieces so they cannot move (they are stalled).
- If neither player can win, the game is a draw.

## KEY RULES & TIPS
- Control the center of the board for maximum mobility.
- Keep pieces on the back row as long as possible to prevent opponent's pieces from being kinged.
- Try to get Kings early — they are significantly more powerful.
- Force exchanges when you have a material advantage.
- Double and triple jumps can swing the game — set them up by sacrificing a piece.
